New laws will give employees more right to flexible working

 Posted on April 2, 2024  |  6 minutes  |  1122 words  |  Aldo Pusey

The next time you find yourself discussing work with colleagues or friends, you may find the conversational touch-points have changed: these days, the hot topic most debated is less likely to be a pay increase or a promotion, but hybrid working.

When the pandemic forced the majority of us who could into setting up offices in our kitchens, businesses realised that working from home no longer equates to slacking off. In fact, studies have shown that on average, those who work from home spend 10 minutes less a day being unproductive, work one more day a week, and are 47 per cent more productive around the clock. This – coupled with business systems that make home working easier, from Zoom to Slack – meant many companies were happy to let their employees continue to work from home for at least a few days a week.

This new way of working has become so ingrained, that fresh government regulations (in effect from 6 April 2024) have been introduced, which give employees the right to request flexible working arrangements from day one of employment – under the previous law, workers needed to be employed for at least 26 weeks before making a request.

While this sounds like a benefit, flexible working expert and co-founder and CEO of Flexa Molly Johnson-Jones says that the reality may not be as clear-cut. The new regulations grant workers the right to request flexible working, “but crucially, there's no guarantee that these requests will be granted,” she says. “This could leave thousands of employees who rely on flexible work in precarious positions if their requests are turned down. Individuals should know whether their needs can be accommodated before they accept a role.”

“The new legislation risks exacerbating “fake flexibility”, where companies claim to offer flexible working on job adverts, but are under no obligation to offer it in practice. This makes it nearly impossible for job seekers to determine which employers will genuinely accommodate their requests for flexible work.”

The push for hybrid working

Johnson-Jones' opinion of many employers being reluctant to allow flexible working in practice is reflected in the attitudes of major businesses, such as Disney, Goldman Sachs and Tesla, which are asking employees to revert to a pre-pandemic, five-days-a-week office schedule. The move has not been popular – new data compiled by Space32, a co-working company, has revealed that almost a quarter of Britons (23 per cent) would turn down their dream job if it required them to return to the office full-time.

Those who work from home are 47 per cent more productive

This is “a striking indication that those pushing for a full-time office return are fighting a losing battle,” says Jon Dweck, CEO and founder co-founder of Space32. “For younger generations in particular, who are at an earlier stage in their careers, going into the office full-time should not be a prerequisite for success. Employers must ditch this one-size-fits-all approach and acknowledge that employee attitudes have shifted.”

A quick straw-poll of my friends supports Dweck’s findings. “I don’t think I could ever go back to working in an office full-time,” says Caroline, a 33-year-old marketing executive. “I think I’m more productive when I work from home a few days a week. It means I have a more balanced lifestyle, too, which means I take fewer days off due to sickness or holidays.” Milly, 38, a lawyer, agrees: “So many companies are offering really attractive hybrid working packages now. It would take an incredible opportunity to persuade me to go back to an office five days a week.”

The benefits of working from home – and the downsides

According to Helen Cowan, founder of coaching company The Tall Wall, the impact of the pandemic on working culture can’t be overstated. “It changed what we want from work, what the boundaries of work and home life can and should be, and the importance of finding our ‘purpose’ – what we’re working for, and what we want from life,” she says.

It's important to consider the benefits and downsides of home-working. Yes, you may feel you have more focused, uninterrupted time to work on solo projects, greater scope to weave admin tasks into your day (putting on a load of laundry during your lunch break, for example) and more flexibility when it comes to responsibilities like childcare. However, working from home can pose a risk to career progression: joining an impromptu meeting at work or going out for a few drinks with your team on a Friday is often a great way to showcase your value, and receive informal mentoring. It’s why a large number of people – 44 per cent in the UK, in fact – favour a hybrid system which enables them to get the best of both worlds.

How to ask for flexible working

If your company is resistant, how do you go about requesting a hybrid working pattern? The new regulations should make this easier, but companies may still be reticent to comply. “Barriers still exist: a LinkedIn study last year found that more than half (52 per cent) of women say a lack of flexibility at work has pushed them to leave, or consider leaving, a job,” says Olivia Bath, career coach and founder of The Women’s Vault. “Research what type of flexible arrangements are already in place in your organisation, and remember that negotiating flexibility is effectively negotiating the terms of your contract.”

Cowan and Bath agree companies that don’t offer flexible working are likely to miss out on good candidates

Cowan agrees that you need to go into any negotiation well-prepared and ready to answer any tricky questions about performance and contact hours. “Think commercially and pragmatically from your employer’s point of view,” she advises. “An effective strategy is to propose a trial approach for three months and offer a check-in every month, to see how the set-up is working for all concerned. Few managers can deny a trial that is measured, monitored and has a potential get-out clause at the end.”

Many companies may want their workforce to return to the office – but when hybrid working has been proved to be effective, employees have a powerful say. “This new data demonstrates that flexible working is a fundamental requirement these days, not a nice add-on,” says Cowan. “If flexible working isn’t part of the package, the job is simply not a dream job for many people. That’s a fundamental shift that is here to stay.”
