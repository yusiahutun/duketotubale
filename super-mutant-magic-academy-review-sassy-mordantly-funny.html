SuperMutant Magic Academy review sassy and mordantly funny

 Posted on February 15, 2024  |  3 minutes  |  572 words  |  Jenniffer Sheldon
Graphic novel of the monthComics and graphic novelsReviewThis pleasingly fat anthology of Jillian Tamaki’s online comic about teenagers with paranormal powers exudes an irresistible wit

This month’s graphic novel is a little weird in places. What do I mean by weird? Only that it doesn’t always work. The majority of its strips are sassy, mordantly funny and feel true in ways that most other depictions of teenage angst simply don’t. But every so often, one will fall completely flat, climaxing in a baffling non-joke that leaves you scratching your head. Still, don’t be put off. SuperMutant Magic Academy, an anthology of a comic that has been published online since 2010, is a pleasingly fat book. All you have to do is turn the page: another gag will be along any second.

Its author, the New Yorker illustrator Jillian Tamaki, is best known for Skim and This One Summer, the award-winning young adult graphic novels she created with her cousin, Mariko Tamaki. SuperMutant Magic Academy, a kind of Hogwarts-Gossip Girl mash-up in which the characters possess various superhero-ish and paranormal powers, is more sketchily drawn than either of those two, but what it lacks in visual loveliness it more than makes up for in wit. In the end, its characters’ peculiar talents matter vastly less than their age. In essence, this is a book about raging hormones – think existential crises, black moods, impossible crushes and extreme lethargy – that just happens to come with a little magic on the side. (One character is too lazy to get up off the sofa and grab his wand, for all that he’s longing to use it to conjure up some nachos and guacamole.)

Like any good school story, SuperMutant Teenage Academy comes with a core cast of regular characters, each one representative of a certain type but with kinks and prickles that are all their own. So meet Wendy, who has cat ears; Marsha, the grumpy, bespectacled friend who carries a secret torch for her; Cheddar, an annoyingly impervious jock; Trixie, a dinosaur girl; Everlasting Boy, who is immortal; and Ms Grimdorff, a waspish teacher who looks like she’s stepped straight off the set of Wicked. (Also in the Magic Academy classes: a cat in a school uniform.) Together, these students try out various roles for size, posturing wildly, sending up both each other and the adults who circumscribe their lives. Labouring as they must under the burden of their parents’ expectations – as mutants go, this lot are quite middle class – they’re brilliantly sarcastic, a spiky manner being the best defence in the endless battle not to appear dorky (though Marsha is out and proud as a geek).

In one strip, a favourite of mine, Wendy asks a girl called Belinda how she learned to roller skate so beautifully. “Oh, my mom enrolled me in lessons when I was three,” she replies, twirling an elegant figure of eight across the yard. “Wow!” says Wendy. “You’re incredible. Just beautiful!” Belinda continues to whoosh and spin. “Thanks, Wendy,” she says. And then: “But I assure you, I’m crying inside.” If this kind of stuff makes you laugh as much as it does me, then SuperMutant Magic Academy may just be the comic for you, whether you’re teenaged or middle-aged.
